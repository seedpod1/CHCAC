What is Cyber Insurance?

Definition: Cyber insurance is a specialized insurance product designed to protect healthcare organizations from internet-based risks, data breaches, and technology-related incidents that can compromise patient data and operational integrity.

First-Party Coverage

Covers direct losses to your organization:

  • Business interruption
  • Data recovery costs
  • Ransom payments
  • Crisis management
  • Forensic investigation

Third-Party Coverage

Covers liability to others:

  • Legal defense costs
  • Regulatory fines (HIPAA)
  • Notification costs
  • Credit monitoring
  • Privacy liability claims

Why Third-Party Attacks Are Surging

Attack Advantages for Criminals

  • One breach = many victims: Compromise one vendor, access hundreds of health centers
  • Weaker security: Vendors often have fewer resources for cybersecurity
  • Trusted access: Vendors bypass perimeter defenses with legitimate credentials
  • Limited visibility: You can't monitor vendor networks
  • Shared responsibility confusion: Gaps in who secures what

Important Sublimits & Policy Terms

‚ö†Ô∏è Warning: Many policies have sublimits that cap specific coverages below the main policy limit. Always review these carefully!

Critical Sublimits to Negotiate

Coverage Area Common Sublimit Recommended Minimum
Ransomware/Extortion $250K-$500K $1M or policy limit
Business Interruption $500K or 60 days $1M or policy limit
Contingent Business Interruption $100K-$250K $500K-$2M (CRITICAL!)
Breach Response (IR/Legal) $250K $500K-$1M
Regulatory Fines/Penalties $100K-$250K $1M or policy limit
Privacy / Wrongful Collection $100K-$250K $1M or policy limit
Social Engineering Fraud $100K $250K minimum

Also Watch For:

  • Waiting Periods: Business interruption often has 8-24 hour waiting period
  • CBI Waiting Period: Should be <8 hours given critical vendor dependencies
  • Retroactive Date: Can exclude coverage for known vulnerabilities
  • Definition of "Ransomware": Some exclude data-only extortion
  • Make sure business interruption includes coverage for systems failure (both BI and CBI)
  • Make sure wrongful collection is defined and includes actions by employees and subcontractors
  • Named vs. Unnamed Vendors: CBI should cover unnamed third parties

How to Present Yourself at Renewal After a Claim

The Remediation Narrative

Carriers want to see that you learned from the incident and are now a better risk. Structure your renewal narrative in three parts:

Part 1: Transparent Accountability

"Here's what happened and what we learned"

  • Brief, factual summary of incident
  • Root cause analysis
  • Security gaps identified
  • Acknowledgment of what should have been in place
  • Tone: Professional, candid, learning-focused

Part 2: Comprehensive Remediation

"Here's exactly what we fixed and when"

  • Timeline of improvements with specific dates
  • Before/After comparison of security posture
  • Evidence: Screenshots, vendor contracts, assessment reports
  • Third-party validation: Security assessments, penetration tests
  • Investment amount: Show financial commitment to security

Part 3: Ongoing Commitment

"Here's our security program going forward"

  • Board/leadership commitment to cybersecurity
  • Annual security budget established
  • Quarterly security meetings scheduled
  • Ongoing monitoring and assessment program
  • Security roadmap for next 12-24 months
